This was a "correct-chalk" slate: the winning edge was rostering several *justified* moderate-own plays *as a connected core*, not fading the board.

Our portfolio held single legs of that core scattered across ten lineups — **no single lineup carried more than 2 of the winning 6.** Max diversification made assembling the correct correlated core structurally impossible. The lone deep finish (#69) came from the one lineup that paired the sub-10% spike with two correct mid-tier ceiling plays — validating both the spike read and the ceiling rule.

### Process Lessons

1. **Correct-chalk-concentration leak (new pattern — near-inverse of Anchor-Equivalence).** On a slate where 3-4 justified moderate-own plays form the obvious-but-correct core, single legs spread across the portfolio don't capture the win — *one lineup has to roster the full core together*. The winning structure was a connected 4-play core; our max overlap was 2 of 6 in any single lineup. Anchor-Equivalence says *split* equivalent chalk across lineups; this says *concentrate* the correct correlated core in at least one. Both are pre-lock checks; they apply to different slate shapes (equivalent-pair coin-flip vs. shared-core birdie-fest).

2. **Anchored the wrong chalk.** Our two heaviest exposures were the field's two highest-owned plays (the ~30-34% own tier), both of which delivered only at-projection. The chalk that actually paid — the ~13-15% own ceiling tier — sat at 1/10 exposure. Paying full field ownership on the ceiling-light chalk while underweighting the justified-ceiling chalk is anti-leverage (Philosophy rule #1: play good chalk, but build around it *sharply* — own the chalk that has ceiling, not the chalk that's merely popular).

3. **Over-diversification capped the portfolio.** Ten bullets touched ~37 distinct golfers. Spreading that wide guarantees no lineup assembles the correct core and clusters the bulk of the portfolio just under the cash line (four lineups at ~233 vs a 238 cash line). With a clear birdie-fest read, fewer distinct cores rostered more deeply beats max breadth.

### What Went Right

- **The sub-10% spike read worked.** Both day-defining spikes (sub-10% own, 50+ FPTS) were on our radar and rostered in 2/10 each. The ceiling-slot process is sound.
- **#69 of 10,701 (top 0.65%).** The one lineup that paired the spike with two correct mid-tier ceiling plays reached real contention — proof the construction process can hit; the failure was portfolio-level, not per-lineup.
- **60+ ceiling rule held** in the lineups that scored — every contending lineup had its ceiling slot doing real work.

### Key Takeaway

This was a correct-chalk birdie-fest, and the leak was structural, not analytical: we identified the right spikes and the right ceiling plays, then *diluted them to death* across ten lineups while overweighting the field's ceiling-light popular chalk. On a slate where the field shares a justified core, the win comes from concentrating that core in at least one lineup — not from owning single legs of it everywhere. New pre-lock check (Correct-Chalk Concentration) added to framework; this is the complement to Anchor-Equivalence, applied to a different slate shape.

## PGA Championship 2026

**Result:** 1 of 3 contests cashed. All three contests were effectively SE (C1: 295-entry SE, 187/295, 194.10 pts, missed by ~30; C2: 1,784-entry SE, 1214/1784, 193.05 pts, missed by ~31; C3: 2,353-entry multi-entry treated as SE, 383/2353, 227.55 pts, **cashed by ~7**). Winning lineups: C1 288.15, C2 299.00, C3 294.65.

**Slate Read:** Major championship R4 at Quail Hollow played as birdie-fest. The slate's defining shots: a sub-5% cheap-spike-of-the-week play that scored 70+ (T-10 starter at low ownership), and a mid-tier ceiling hit at 15-20% ownership. All three contest winners across the three different contests had this same dual-ceiling-shot pattern (sub-5% spike + mid-tier ceiling).

**The Single Decisive Miss / Hit:** Zero exposure across all 3 lineups to the sub-5% cheap-spike-of-the-week play. This was a structural floor violation that hit every lineup regardless of which slate read each was built around. Conversely, the lone cash (C3) came from being the only lineup with the mid-tier ceiling shot — validating the ceiling-player rule.

### Process Lessons

1. **Diversification of slate reads is valid for low-conviction slates; skipping structural floor on any lineup is not.** Three contests, three different reads (Mode B — diversified multi-read) was a defensible hedge against an uncertain slate. The killer was not the diversification — it was that none of the three lineups hit the sub-5% spike slot, which is mandatory on any potential birdie-fest slate. Mode B is a hedge against uncertain reads, not a license to skip rules on individual lineups. (Codified as Multi-Contest Slate Read Discipline section in framework — Mode A vs Mode B with mandatory structural floor in both.)

2. **Analyst anchor bias is a real leak — and it applies to Claude's autopsy framing as well.** A player featured in 2 of 3 lineups (scored 30.55 — moderate) was a player the analyst (Claude) had discussed favorably the prior week (Truist autopsy). The Truist autopsy framing carried into this build without re-validating against the new slate. **Fix on the analyst side:** autopsies reference *patterns that worked* (cheap-spike-of-the-week profile, sub-25 climber on birdie-fest), not *specific players who worked*. Specific player names in autopsies become default leans the following week. (Codified in Philosophy "What I Push Back On" section.)

3. **The mid-tier 10-20% ownership trap.** Stacking 3-4 plays in the 10-20% own range creates field-weight exposure without leverage. Either commit higher (20%+ on real ceiling shots) or rotate to sub-5% spike plays. The middle is the trap. (Codified in framework: max 2 plays at 10-20% ownership per lineup.)

4. **Small-field multi-entry contests are functionally SE.** A 2,353-entry contest with 18-max entry cap was correctly treated as a single-entry-style build. Contest size and dynamics determine the approach; the DraftKings label does not. (Codified in framework Contest Size Calibration note.)

5. **The sub-5% spike sub-clause to the ceiling-player rule.** Every lineup had a nominal 60+ ceiling player (top anchors and elite mid-tier names). But on major championship / birdie-fest slates, every contest winner included a sub-5% cheap-spike-of-the-week play. The rule should be read as: every lineup needs at least one realistic 60+ ceiling player, AND on birdie-fest / major slates, the portfolio should contain at least one sub-5% spike play across the build. (Codified in framework Ceiling-Player Requirement section.)

### What Went Right

- **C3 cashed.** The mid-tier ceiling shot at 15-20% ownership was the difference. The lineup hit 4 of 6 plays with that play delivering 65+ points.
- **Modal anchor call in C1** — correctly field-weighted at 37% ownership. He delivered top score on that lineup.

### Key Takeaway

The PGA Championship loss was not a failure of slate-read diversification (Mode B can be the right move on uncertain slates). The loss was that none of the three lineups hit the structural floor on the sub-5% spike slot — a structural rule violation that hit every lineup regardless of which read it was built around. The correct workflow going forward: Stage 1 (slate read), Stage 2 (build canonical or multi-read), Stage 3 (pressure test each lineup against structural floor), Stage 4 (deploy). The Kitayama-equivalent miss was a Stage 3 failure — the pressure test would have caught zero sub-5% spike across the portfolio. Build first, pressure-test second is non-negotiable.

## Truist Championship 2026

**Result:** 0 of 4 lineups cashed. Best finish: #1729 of 7,134 (LU3 — 249.15 pts vs ~250 cash line, missed by 19 places). Winning lineup scored 334.55.

**Slate Read:** Predicted tough-course rules (analyst was firmly committed, framework decision inputs agreed); slate played as clear birdie-fest. Cheap-spike-of-the-week profile players (way-back T-25+ starters at sub-5% ownership) posted 50-65+ scores. Coffin block players (high-owned mid-tier with marginal profiles) all delivered 45+ pts. Top-1 lineup scored 334.55 with a build heavy on sub-25 climbers and birdie-fest chalk.

**The Single Decisive Miss:** Removing two cheap-spike-of-the-week plays from the final LU1 revision, citing "starting position math" that concluded T32/T41 starters couldn't realistically score 60+. The math implicitly assumed tough-course scoring. On the actual birdie-fest, those plays would have moved LU1 from #3763 to ~top-300 (cashes comfortably). The original build was correct; the revision was over-optimization within a wrong frame.

### Process Lessons

1. **Rule-set splitting becomes the default for 4-bullet portfolios, not the exception.** Two consecutive R4s lost at the rule-set commitment level — Cadillac with an uncertain analyst, Truist with a certain analyst. Analyst confidence is not sufficient evidence; the analyst is reading the same noisy signals we are. New default: 2-2 split on rule-set unless there's overwhelming forcing evidence (severe weather, course setup change, scoring distribution that physically can't go the other way).

2. **Ceiling math is rule-set-dependent. Name the assumption every time.** "T32 starter needs a historic round to score 60+" is true on tough course (winning at -15 to -17) and false on birdie-fest (winning at -19 to -22). Cheap-spike-of-the-week profile plays have radically different equity by rule-set. Before removing a sub-5% punt for "limited ceiling," check whether the ceiling-math assumes the rule-set you're not certain about.

3. **Coffin block identification is rule-set-dependent.** Three "coffin" plays all hit 45+ pts. On birdie-fest the chalk often hits because cum own targets are naturally higher (113%) and concentration represents accurate ownership on real value. Coffin avoidance is a tough-course rule. On birdie-fest, the "coffin block" can become the "winning block." Don't apply universally.

4. **Anchor-equivalence pattern repeats.** Analyst-named pick at 20% own vs uncommented same-profile alternative at 16% own — same salary tier, same starting position. We had the named pick at 25%, zero exposure to the alternative. Named scored 43.85, alternative scored 64.4. The Cadillac autopsy lesson is correct but we're missing implementations of it during the build. **The pre-lock check needs to be explicit: for every analyst-named pick at sub-25% ownership, identify the lower-owned same-profile alternative and verify exposure.**

### What Went Right

- **Anchor-equivalent rotation in LU3 — Cadillac lesson applied successfully.** Sub-10% analyst-avoided alternative was rotated to 25% exposure against analyst "avoid" call, citing Cadillac. Scored 41.6 pts. LU3 was our best finish directly because of this rotation. The Cadillac autopsy lesson is working — we now need to apply the same discipline at every anchor-equivalent pair, not just the obvious ones.
- **Low-owned ceiling shot at 50% exposure** — correct overweight, hit 62.6 pts.
- **2 of 4 sub-5% picks delivered ceiling scores** — validates the sub-5% leverage approach when applied to right-profile plays.

### Key Takeaway

The recurring failure mode is rule-set over-commitment based on analyst certainty. Two slates in a row, the rule-set call was wrong and we lost the entire build at that decision point regardless of how clean the downstream construction was. The new default is mandatory 2-2 rule-set splitting on 4-bullet portfolios. The secondary takeaway is that mid-build revisions need to preserve the original ceiling exposures unless the new replacement has provably equal or better ceiling under both rule-sets — not just the assumed one. The original build had the right cheap-spike-of-the-week leverage; the revision lost it by anchoring on tough-course ceiling math.

## Cadillac Championship 2026

**Result:** 4 of 4 lineups missed cash. Best finish: #6320 of 9,512 (275.05 pts vs 303.8 cash line).

**Slate Read:** Predicted tough-course rules; tournament played as birdie-fest. Multiple mid-tier guys posted big rounds; the lower-priced anchor-equivalent of the named structural value play was the slate-winning add.

**The Single Decisive Miss:** The slate-winning play was a same-profile alternative to the analyst-named structural value anchor. Same salary tier, same ownership, same starting position band. We concentrated 75% on the named pick. The field bet on the alternative. Different result by 53 points.

### Process Lessons

1. **The Anchor-Equivalence Rule.** For every analyst-named "structural value" anchor, identify the 1-2 equivalent-profile alternatives at same salary/ownership/LB-position tier and rotate exposure. Don't concentrate on the named pick when the field has access to equivalent-profile clones.

2. **Never Zero Out a Top-3 Anchor With 4 Bullets.** Going under-weight on the modal stack at 25% (1 of 4) is leverage. Going to 0% when the leader is up 4+ shots is a voluntary lottery exit. The modal anchor at 0% across all 4 cost us this slate.

3. **Split Portfolio When Rule-Set Read Is Uncertain.** When the analyst himself flags he "went back and forth" between birdie-fest vs tough-course, commit to neither read with full bullets. Split 2-2 on a 4-bullet portfolio. Cost of being wrong on the foundational read with full commitment is too high.

4. **The 60+ Ceiling Rule.** No lineup without at least one player with a realistic 60+ point ceiling can win a 5K+ entrant Showdown. None of our 4 lineups had a 60+ ceiling player. The math doesn't work without one.

5. **"Safe" Anchors Aren't GPP Anchors.** The named structural value anchor at 11% own felt like leverage because of name recognition, but his profile (T-23 starter, ball-striker on a course rewarding birdies) was the lowest-ceiling profile in the $9K range. Picking floor in a contest that pays ceiling.

### What Went Right (Don't Throw The Process Out)

- Sub-10% conviction play fired (correct exposure on a profile that worked)
- Analyst-blessed mid-tier hit at 25% exposure
- Cheap LB punt worked at 25% exposure
- **Coffin avoidance was correct.** Multiple 25%+ ownership players all underperformed price. Avoiding the coffin block saved us from being deeper in the field.

### Key Takeaway

The process at the punt-slot level was solid. The failure was at the **anchor and rule-set levels** — the two highest-leverage decisions of the build. Both decisions were defensible in isolation but compounded into an unrecoverable structural deficit. Next time: more humility on rule-set commitment, more discipline on anchor-profile rotation.
