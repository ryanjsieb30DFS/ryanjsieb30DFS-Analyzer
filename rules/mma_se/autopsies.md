## 2026-05-09 — UFC Chimaev vs. Strickland — SE Review

### Contests Played

| Contest | Buy-in | Field | Cash Line | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $12 SE | $12 | 1,176 | Top ~294 (25%) | Rank 726 (61.9%) — no cash |
| $5 SE | $5 | 1,783 | Top ~445 (25%) | Rank 1,732 (97.7%) — no cash |

Total SE buy-in: $17. Zero return. Net loss: -$17.

### Pre-Slate Beliefs

- **Chimaev was a 100% lock anchor** in both SEs (correctly scored 105.08).
- **Brady was a chalk fade target** before the injury news; both SEs ended up moving toward Buckley post-news.
- **The leverage punt slot was Stephens** in both SEs.

### What Actually Happened

The Brady→Buckley swap killed both SEs. Brady scored the slate's top score (136.27); Buckley scored 5.20. The $5 SE moved to 100% Buckley post-news, sealing its fate.

The $12 SE submission did NOT match the final recommendation (recommended Kopylov→Miller swap for sub-15% leverage). Submitted lineup retained Kopylov. Rank 726 in 1,176-entrant field.

### Decision-Level Errors

**1. $5 SE: Brady→Buckley swap was the catastrophic micro-decision.** Single entry against Buckley's KO-or-bust profile when Brady was the more reliable scorer in *any* outcome was a poor risk-adjusted call. This was a process error — overreaction to speculative news.

**2. $12 SE execution error.** The recommended Kopylov→Miller swap for sub-15% leverage was not applied. Submitted lineup retained Kopylov, resulting in zero sub-15% fighters in the lineup and max ownership concentration with no differentiation in a 1,176-entrant field. This was not a strategy error — it was a submission verification failure.

### What Worked

- **Chimaev lock was correct** in both SEs.
- **Pre-news SE structure was defensible** — both lineups had reasonable conviction anchors with one leverage swing each.

### Process-Level Lessons

1. **Verify SE submissions match final recommendation.** The Kopylov-not-Miller miss in the $12 SE was an execution error, not a strategy error. Added to framework: "Submission verified — read back the locked DK lineup before final submit."

2. **For SE: react fully on confirmed news, partial on speculative.** SE is one shot — playing a compromised fighter against an opponent whose path to victory is exploiting that compromise is bad regardless of news certainty. But the inverse is also true: swapping AWAY from a strong play on speculative news is high-risk in SE.

### What This Slate Was Not

A failure of SE strategy. The framework didn't yet exist — these were SEs built as MME-style lineups with optimizer assistance. The 5/16/26 SE losses prompted the creation of a dedicated SE framework.

## 2026-05-16 — UFC Vegas 117 (Allen vs. Costa) — SE Review

### Contests Played

| Contest | Buy-in | Field | Cash Line | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $12 SE | $12 | 490 | Top 121 (25%) | Rank 295, 362.61 pts — no cash |
| $5 SE | $5 | 1,189 | Top 262 (22%) | Rank 676, 364.85 pts — no cash |

Total SE buy-in: $17. Zero return. Net loss: -$17.

### Pre-Slate Beliefs

- **Santos + Gantt were twin convictions.** Both high-ceiling, both with finish equity. Gantt via wrestling + physicality, Santos via pace + wrestling. Defensible pair.
- **Main event should be diversified.** One SE took Costa side, the other took Allen side. This was correct diversification intent.
- **Veretennikov and Petroski would be the leverage swings.** Sub-20% owned, binary fights, KO/sub equity upside. Correct leverage identification.
- **Vieira and Viana would provide floor.** Both with reliable 80-85 point decision floors in wins. Salary savers.

### What Actually Happened

**$12 SE: Gantt (129) + Santos (30.82) + Erslan (86.33) + Costa (46.75) + Veretennikov (6.60) + Vieira (62.66) = 362.61 pts. Rank 295/490.**

Winning $12 SE score: 660.81 pts.

**$5 SE: Gantt (129) + Santos (30.82) + Petroski (14.28) + Allen (142.84) + Tokkos (24.27) + Viana (23.19) = 364.85 pts. Rank 676/1,189.**

Winning $5 SE score: 651.47 pts.

### Decision-Level Errors

**1. Santos as a common conviction across both SEs was a structural error.** Both SEs locked Santos + Gantt. When Santos lost (30.82 in a loss to Choi), both lineups were penalized identically. The $5 SE's different top-end (Allen/Petroski instead of Erslan/Costa) couldn't overcome the common Santos failure because Santos was only ~30 points. **This violates the SE diversification rule: "DO NOT share both conviction anchors across both lineups."**

Correct approach: One SE has Santos + Gantt, the other has Gantt + Allen (or Gantt + Erslan), forcing one lineup to fade Santos. When Santos loses, the other lineup at least has a different conviction core to lean on.

**2. Veretennikov at $7,900 in $12 SE was a poor leverage choice.** He was sub-20% owned, had KO equity vs. Williams, but he got KO'd (6.60 pts). More problematic: the $12 SE's field size is small (490), which means one good swing play can carry a whole lineup. Veretennikov as a pure KO-dependent bet was too binary. Better leverage on a small field: a fighter with both KO upside AND decision floor (Vieira hit 87 in win, would have been a better lever).

**3. Petroski at $8,900 in $5 SE was expensive for a leverage piece.** At 23% own, Petroski wasn't truly low-owned leverage; he was a secondary conviction play. For a $5 SE in a 1,189-entrant field, spending $8,900 on a 23%-owned fighter as your "leverage" is not true differentiation. Better to lock Gantt + Allen (both chalk), then use the remaining $23K to do something genuinely contrarian — either fade a top chalk or pair two low-owned finishers.

**4. Both SEs had poor ceiling projections.** Highest possible scores for both lineups:
- $12 SE ceiling: Gantt (98.5) + Santos (100.4) + Erslan (95.3) + Costa (98.8) + Veretennikov (91.8) + Vieira (87.0) = 571.8 pts
- $5 SE ceiling: Gantt (98.5) + Santos (100.4) + Petroski (92.6) + Allen (95.5) + Tokkos (97.2) + Viana (96.4) = 580.6 pts

Neither lineup had a realistic path to top-10 even if all 6 hit. We weren't playing to win; we were playing to cash. **In SE, you lock lineups with ceiling cases that could plausibly finish top-10 if variance breaks right. These lineups were ceiling-capped at rank 250-350 in a 490-entrant field.**

### What Worked

- **Gantt conviction was correct.** He scored 129.45, his ceiling case. We locked him in both SEs. That part was right.
- **Main event diversification was correct in principle.** Having one SE on Costa, one on Allen was smart. Unfortunately, Allen won and Costa busted, so the Allen-side $5 SE had a better fight outcome despite losing overall.
- **Vieira floor was real.** Even in a loss, she provided 62.66 points — reliable.

### Process-Level Lessons

1. **Never lock identical conviction cores across multiple SEs.** The 5/16/26 SEs both had Santos + Gantt. This made them a single bet split across two entries rather than two independent bets. When Santos failed, both failed. **Better structure: differentiate on at least one of the two conviction anchors. Accept that this means one SE underweights a play you believe in — that's the point of hedging.**

2. **Small-field SEs (under 500) require high-ceiling leverage plays, not binary-dependent plays.** In a 490-entrant field, Veretennikov was a poor leverage choice because his upside (KO) had only ~25% probability and his downside was a loss (0 pts). Better: a fighter with both KO upside (25% prob, 115 pts) AND decision floor (75% prob, 85 pts) for a blended EV of ~90 pts. Vieira in that spot would have been better — she had 87-point ceiling and she hit it.

3. **Secondary plays (20-30% owned) aren't leverage in SE context.** Petroski at 23% own isn't a lever; it's a secondary chalk play. For SE leverage, you need sub-15% owned, and ideally sub-12%. **Audit your "leverage" play: if it's owned 20%+, it's not leverage, it's just a secondary play. Swap it for something that actually contradicts chalk.**

4. **Ceiling-projections matter in SE.** Before locking a SE, add up all 6 fighters' win-case projections. If the total is under 600 (for a 490-entrant field), the lineup's ceiling is probably not high enough to win. We locked two lineups with ceilings of ~572 and ~581 in a field where the winner was 660.81 and cash was probably ~480. Our ceilings were there; we just didn't hit them.

5. **Santos + Gantt as a pair in SE was the right conviction; locking them in BOTH SEs was the mistake.** If you believe in both, build ONE lineup with them as the core, then build a SECOND lineup that fades one of them (lean on Allen + Erslan instead). This hedges against the scenario where both fail (which didn't happen; Santos failed, Gantt hit) or both hit (good variance). But don't split the pair across two lineups and call it diversification.

6. **Process over outcome: both SEs failed for a specific reason (Santos loss),** not because the process was wrong. The process failures were: (a) common conviction cores, (b) weak leverage plays relative to field size. The outcome failure was Santos losing to Choi, which both lineups couldn't overcome because Santos only scored 30.82.

### Process-Level Changes To Framework

Added to SE framework:
- "Never lock identical conviction cores across multiple SEs" — diversification rule for two-entry slates
- "Ceiling threshold discipline" — sum win-case projections before locking, compare to field-size threshold
- "Binary-dependent leverage plays" — pure KO-or-bust leverage is structurally weak in small fields
- "Secondary plays are not leverage" — 20%+ owned plays don't differentiate

### What This Slate Was Not

A success that validates SE process. Both SEs lost. But the losses were instructive — they showed exactly what the SE framework should prevent: identical conviction cores across multiple entries, and leverage pieces that are too binary-dependent for small fields.

The lesson is not "SEs are bad." The lesson is "these two SEs were built without proper SE discipline." They were constructed as "two MME lineups" rather than "two SE lineups with hedged conviction cores."

## 2026-05-30 — UFC Macau — SE / multi-contest Review

### Contests Played

| Contest | Field | Cash (approx) | Best result |
|---|---|---|---|
| $2K Sprawl (3-max) | 577 | top ~20% | **L3 — 536.76 → rank 30 (top 5.2%)** ✅ |
| $5K Clinch (SE) | 901 | top ~25% | L1 (Zhang) — 339.26 → rank 539 (61%, no cash) |
| $100K MEGA (10 of 150-max) | 34,579 | top ~22% | L3 — 536.76 → rank 1,681 (top 4.9%) |

**Winning lineup (690.21):** Pavlovich 128.6 · Haddon 128.1 · Menifield 127.2 · Vera 114.6 · Amorim 101.3 · Hill 90.4.
**Best user lineup L3 (536.76):** Pavlovich · Haddon · Menifield · Dias 108.5 · **Ding 25.2 · Harris 19.2**. Shared 3 of 6 with the winner; lost the slate on the two forced mid-range pieces.

### What Actually Happened
The two emphasized top anchors hit huge — **Pavlovich (128.6)** and **Haddon (128.1)**, Brett's highest-confidence "doesn't need a KO" chalk pivot. The mandated Zhang-fade leverage piece **Menifield (127.2 at 15.9% own)** was a top-3 slate score. But the chalkiest play on the board, **Zhang (44% own, KO-or-bust) busted to 10.09**, and the two heavily-stacked mid-range "ceiling" pieces died: **Ding (70% portfolio exposure → 25.2)** and **Perez (50% → 7.2)**. The actual winner used none of the high-projection mid-range — it filled around Pavlovich/Haddon/Menifield with the secondary tier we rated "bust risk / mix-in only": **Amorim (101, 0% of our exposure)** and **Vera (114, 10%)**.

### What Worked
- The **anchor-equivalence rule saved the slate** (5th validation). Every good finish was a Pavlovich-not-Zhang lineup; L3 (Pavlovich+Haddon double-anchor) went 30/577 and 1,681/34,579.
- Haddon-as-non-KO-dependent-chalk-pivot read was exactly right.
- Matthews ceiling-cap fade (0%) and Figueiredo qualitative fade were both correct (Matthews won but capped at 89.9; Figgy 11.25).

### Decision-Level Errors
1. **Equal 40/40 Zhang/Pavlovich weighting.** Zhang was *both* the chalkiest play *and* KO-or-bust — the pivot side should have been weighted heavier. 40% of the portfolio rode the bust.
2. **Wrong SE bullet.** The single SE used L1 (Zhang chalk-correct) → 539/901. L3 (the differentiated Pavlovich+Haddon build) was the stronger lineup and proved it elsewhere.
3. **Ceiling-threshold tunnel vision.** The ≥620 win-case gate stacked Dias/Ding/Perez (70/70/50%) and zeroed out the cheap secondary leverage (Amorim, Vera) that actually won.

### Process-Level Lessons (codified into framework.md)
- **Asymmetric anchor-equivalence weighting** when the chalk side is chalkiest-on-slate AND KO-or-bust.
- **SE bullet = highest-conviction *differentiated* build**, not the chalk-correct one, when the chalk anchor is boom/bust.
- **Win-case ceiling under-rates low-own finishers** — reserve ≥1 lineup for the field-fade secondary tier; don't let the ceiling gate force out cheap secondary leverage.

### What This Slate Was Not
A bust. The process got the leverage core (Pavlovich/Haddon/Menifield) dead right and produced a top-5% finish in two contests. The gap to the optimal was concentrated in three correctable weighting decisions, not a broken read.
