Operational process for constructing and playing Single Entry lineups. SEs are built manually, not via optimizer.

## Pre-Slate Workflow

### Step 1 — Conviction Play Identification

Before building, identify your 2-3 conviction anchors for the slate. These are fighters where:
- Your confidence in their win equity is >55% (meaningfully ahead of field or aligned with field consensus on a strong play)
- The fighter has either ceiling equity (KO/sub potential) OR floor equity (reliable scoring in a win)
- You would be comfortable having 70%+ of a lineup's score depend on them

Write these down before building. Do not add or subtract mid-build.

**Examples of conviction anchors:**
- A -300 favorite with clear dominance + a high pain point for the opponent
- A sub-30%-owned play with strong qualitative case (finish equity, matchup edge) despite lower projection
- A high-ceiling play where both the projection and qualitative read agree (rare; treasure these)

**Examples of NOT-conviction anchors:**
- A fighter at 45% ownership where the field is already locked in
- A coin-flip decision play (50% win, no finish equity)
- A play you're including just because they fit salary

### Step 2 — Contrarian Swing Identification

Identify one leverage swing play that:
- Is sub-20% owned (preferably sub-15%)
- Has a realistic path to a big score (100+ points)
- Contradicts chalk or fills a gap in chalk

This is your edge. One play per SE. Not more.

**Audit your "leverage" play:** if it's owned 20%+, it's not leverage, it's just a secondary play. Swap it for something that actually contradicts chalk.

### Step 3 — Field-Size Calibration

Check the field size. Are you playing in a 490-entrant, 1,200-entrant, or 3,000-entrant contest?

- **Small field (<500):** You can be slightly more chalk-leaning. One good conviction pair + one leverage swing is enough. Avoid pure-binary leverage plays (KO-or-bust) — prefer plays with both ceiling and floor equity.
- **Medium field (500-1,500):** You need genuine differentiation. Conviction core is 60-65%, leverage is 35-40%.
- **Large field (1,500+):** You need significant differentiation. Consider a lineup that fades a top-chalk play or pairs two contrarian pieces.

### Step 4 — Manual Lineup Construction

Build the lineup by hand. Do not run it through the optimizer. Here's the process:

1. **Lock your 2-3 conviction anchors.** These are your first 2-3 slots (salary-weighted).
2. **Lock your 1 leverage swing.** One slot.
3. **Fill the remaining 2-3 slots with value/support.** These are fighters who:
   - Complement your anchors (fit salary, don't create opponent stacks)
   - Have moderate ownership (15-35%, not chalk, not punts)
   - Have either decent ceiling or reliable floor
   - Are rarely the "hero" of the lineup; they're supporting the anchors

4. **Verify no opponent stacks.** All 6 fighters from different fights.

5. **Verify salary ($48,500-$50,000).**

6. **Calculate ceiling projection.** Sum the "if win" projections for all 6 fighters. Compare to historical winning scores for the field size:
   - 490-entrant field: 660+ pts to win, 480+ to cash
   - 1,189-entrant field: 650+ pts to win, 470+ to cash
   - 3,000+ entrant field: 700+ pts to win, 490+ to cash

7. **Write down your reasoning for each fighter.** Why conviction? Why leverage? Why support?

### Step 5 — Diversification (if playing multiple SEs)

If playing 2 SEs in the same slate:

**DO NOT** share both conviction anchors across both lineups. Pick one of:
- **One has Santos, the other doesn't** (even if both are 50%+ win rate)
- **One plays Erslan/Tokkos, the other plays Williams/Veretennikov** (different binary fight sides)
- **One fades Wellmaker, the other plays him** (major chalk pivot)

This is uncomfortable — it means one SE underweights something you believe in. This is the point. If both SEs have identical conviction cores, they'll fail together.

**The leverage swing can differ**, but it's secondary. The anchor differentiation is what saves you when conviction fails.

## Pre-Submission Checklist for SE

Before locking each lineup:

1. ✓ **2-3 conviction anchors are present and defensible** (written reasoning exists)
2. ✓ **1 leverage swing is present, sub-20% owned, has ceiling case**
3. ✓ **No opponent stacks** across all 6 fighters
4. ✓ **Salary $48,500-$50,000**
5. ✓ **If playing multiple SEs, at least one conviction anchor differs**
6. ✓ **Written reasoning for each fighter exists** (stored somewhere, even a note to yourself)
7. ✓ **Ceiling projection meets field-size threshold:**
   - 490-entrant: ceiling sum > 600
   - 1,189-entrant: ceiling sum > 600
   - 3,000-entrant: ceiling sum > 650
8. ✓ **Expected cash rank** (ask yourself: "If all 6 hit and have their typical win-scores, what rank would this lineup achieve in the field size I'm playing?")
9. ✓ **No more than 1 fighter priced under $7,200** (punt-tier discipline from MME framework)
10. ✓ **Submission verified** — read back the locked DK lineup before final submit (5/9 execution lesson)

If any check fails, revise before submitting.

## SE-Specific Decision Framework

### When to Play a SE

Play a SE when:
- You have 2-3 genuine conviction plays (>55% confidence each)
- The slate has at least one clear leverage piece (sub-20% owned, ceiling case)
- You have enough bankroll to absorb a loss without changing your MME process
- The field size is suitable for your bankroll (small fields <$20 buyin, large fields <$10 buyin)

### When NOT to Play a SE

Don't play a SE when:
- Your "conviction" plays are really just "plays I like moderately"
- The leverage piece requires multiple other outcomes to align (too much variance)
- You're playing multiple SEs with identical conviction cores (you're wasting entries)
- You're low on bankroll and need every dollar for MME

### The Santos/Gantt Pairing Lesson (5/16/26)

The 5/16/26 SEs both locked Santos + Gantt as common convictions. This was correct as a conviction pair — both had strong cases. But both SEs failed identically when Santos lost, because the other 4 fighters couldn't compensate.

**Better approach if you believe in both:** Build ONE SE with Santos + Gantt + leverage stack, then build a SECOND SE with Gantt + DIFFERENT conviction (Allen, Erslan, etc.) + different leverage. This way, when Santos fails in the first lineup, the second lineup has a different anchor that might hit.

Or: Believe in only ONE of the pair. Pick Gantt as the conviction anchor, accept that it means running WITHOUT Santos in one lineup even though you like him. One lineup can't do everything.

### Binary-Dependent Leverage Plays (5/16/26)

In small-field SEs (under 500 entrants), pure-binary leverage plays (KO-or-bust strikers) are structurally weak. The Veretennikov pick in $12 SE was sub-20% owned with KO equity, but his expected outcome distribution was: 25% chance of KO (115 pts), 75% chance of loss (0 pts). Blended EV ~29 pts.

A better leverage piece for the same salary slot would have been a fighter with KO upside AND decision floor — e.g., Vieira at 87-pt ceiling with reliable scoring across multiple paths to win. The lever should differentiate AND have a realistic non-ceiling outcome.

**Rule:** For small fields, leverage plays should have blended EV >50 pts. Pure-binary plays go in large fields where higher variance pays better.

### Secondary Plays Are Not Leverage

If your "leverage" play is owned 20%+, it's not leverage — it's a secondary chalk play. True leverage in SE context means sub-15% owned, ideally sub-12%. Petroski at 23% own in the $5 SE was not actually contrarian; he was just less-chalky chalk.

### Finish-Capable Favorites Are Not Secondary Chalk (7/12/26 — 3-slate validated)

Ownership alone does not make a mid-priced favorite a leak. EXEMPT from the "trim the
20-25% chalk" instinct any **finish-capable favorite in a clean matchup** — a real
finishing (or dominant-decision) path at mid ownership is a legitimate high-exposure
core, structurally distinct from a no-ceiling decision-grinder at the same ownership.
Screen the piece, not the number: a clean finish/dominance path → core; a capped,
decision-dependent grinder → trimmable.

Evidence (3 slates): 6/20 Oliveira (25% own, flagged "trim to 3-4") went 122.51 and
anchored the field winner + the user's best build. 6/27 Abus (17%, 105.08) and Camilo
(18-20%, 101.69) were slate-definers. 7/12 UFC 329 Pinas (26%, 110.7), Gandra (29-33%,
104.2) and Green (28-35%, 120.1) were three mid-own finish-cores, all in the 686.38
winner. Trimming any as "secondary chalk" would have cut the winning spine each slate.

### Asymmetric Anchor-Equivalence Weighting (5/30/26 — UFC Macau)

The Anchor-Equivalence rule (run the pivot in ≥1 lineup) is about *presence*, not *weighting*. On 5/30/26 Zhang (44% own — chalkiest on the slate) and Pavlovich were an anchor-equiv pair; the portfolio split them 40%/40%. Zhang was KO-or-bust and busted to 10.09; Pavlovich smashed (128.6). Equal weighting meant 40% of the portfolio rode the play that died.

**Rule:** when the chalk side of an anchor-equiv pair is *both* the chalkiest play on the slate *and* KO-or-bust (no decision floor), weight the **pivot side heavier than 50/50** — the pivot carries the same realistic win-equity with far better leverage. Presence in ≥1 lineup is the floor, not the target.

### SE-Bullet Selection (5/30/26 — UFC Macau)

When you have one SE entry and a multi-lineup portfolio behind it, the SE bullet should be your **highest-conviction *differentiated* build — not the chalk-correct one — whenever the chalk anchor is boom/bust.** On 5/30/26 the lone SE used the Zhang chalk-correct lineup (539/901, no cash); the Pavlovich+Haddon double-anchor build was clearly stronger and went 30/577 in the 3-max. The single bullet is where differentiation matters most, because you only get one swing.

### Win-Case Ceiling Under-Rates Low-Own Finishers (5/30/26 — UFC Macau)

The ≥600–620 ceiling-threshold (summing projector win-cases) is computed from chalk-friendly projections, so it systematically steers the portfolio toward high-projection mid-range chalk and *away* from cheap, low-owned secondary pieces whose win-case the projections under-rate. On 5/30/26 the threshold stacked Dias/Ding/Perez (70/70/50%) — two of which busted — while the actual winner's differentiators were **Amorim (101 pts, we ran 0%) and Vera (114 pts, 10%)**, both rated "bust risk / mix-in only."

**Rule:** keep the ceiling threshold, but **reserve ≥1 lineup for the field-fade secondary tier** — cheap, sub-25%-owned fighters with a live finishing/volume path whose projection under-states their ceiling. Don't let the ceiling gate zero out the pieces that actually separate a winning lineup from the chalk field.

## Post-SE Review

After every slate, if you played a SE:

1. **Did the lineup cash?**
   - Yes: Compare your rank to the spread. Top 20? Top 5? Top 1? Document what worked.
   - No: By how much did you miss? Rank 100 of 500 (cash 50)? Rank 250 of 500 (no cash)? This matters for the lesson.

2. **Which of your 6 fighters hit their ceiling / floor / missed entirely?**
   - Conviction anchors: Did they perform to expectation?
   - Leverage swing: Did it hit or bust?
   - Support pieces: Did they do their job?

3. **Was the lineup's failure due to process or variance?**
   - Process error: You locked a fighter who had a bad matchup you missed, or you built without conviction anchors, or you had a stupid opponent stack.
   - Variance: All 6 fighters played their game fine but the slate broke in a way your lineup didn't anticipate.

4. **Did the lever swing hit while the conviction anchors failed?** This is a valuable lesson — it means your contrarian was right and your conviction was wrong. Adjust.

5. **If playing multiple SEs and both lost identically, what was the common failure point?** (This happened on 5/16/26 with Santos.)

6. **Update SE_mma_autopsies with the slate-specific lessons.**

## SE vs. MME

SEs are NOT:
- A backup when MME struggles
- A way to "diversify" your bankroll
- An alternative format for the same game

SEs ARE:
- A different skill to develop separately
- A higher-variance, higher-upside format
- Valuable only if you commit to learning them over 10+ slates

If you're going to play SEs, play them seriously. If you're not serious, skip them and focus on MME where the framework is mature.
