# MMA DFS Philosophy — SE (Single Entry)

**Contest Type:** Single Entry (SE)
**Document Type:** Core Philosophy
**Last Updated:** May 17, 2026
**Companion Files:** SE_mma_framework_2026-05-17.md, SE_mma_autopsies_2026-05-17.md

---

Core beliefs specific to Single Entry contests. These complement the general MMA DFS philosophy but apply the core principles to a fundamentally different contest structure.

## The SE Win Condition

Single Entry contests are **binary: you win or you don't.** There is no portfolio theory, no diversification across 150 lineups, no hedge against being wrong on one fighter. A SE is one lineup, one outcome, one shot.

**This changes everything about how to approach the slate.**

The MME GPP philosophy says "build for ceiling, not floor." SE flips this: **build for the highest-probability ceiling, not the unlikely ceiling.** In a 490-entrant SE, you need 5-6 fighters to hit *and* to hit at high leverage. The ceiling case must be both realistic (>15% probability) and yours (high differentiation on that specific outcome path).

A 150-lineup MME can afford to have 30 lineups that are "all-in on Veretennikov KOs Williams." A SE cannot. Your one lineup must have a reasonable path to winning that doesn't rely on a single contrarian bet paying off.

## Conviction vs. Contrarian in SE

**Conviction plays in SE are non-negotiable.** You lock at least 2-3 fighters you believe will hit the slate. These are your anchors — the plays where your analysis (qualitative or projection-based) is aligned with the field or ahead of the field.

**Contrarian plays in SE are leverage, not the core bet.** You include exactly one contrarian swing play. This is your edge — the play where the field is wrong and you're right. But it's supporting a conviction core, not carrying the whole lineup.

The difference from MME: In MME, you can have lineups that are 60% conviction, 40% contrarian (or vice versa). In SE, the ratio is roughly 70% conviction, 30% contrarian. You're making one shot and you need it to work — conviction plays are the load-bearing walls.

## Field Size Matters More in SE

A 490-entrant SE is a fundamentally different game from a 1,189-entrant SE, which is different from a 3,000-entrant SE.

- **Under 500 entries:** The field is small enough that a single high-leverage lineup can place top-5. Differentiation matters, but a well-constructed conviction core can still hit hard.
- **500-1,500 entries:** The "sweet spot" for SE. Large enough that you need differentiation; small enough that differentiation pays.
- **1,500+ entries:** You need significant differentiation to cash. The chalk is more locked in. Leverage is mandatory, not optional.

A $12 SE (490 entries) and $5 SE (1,189 entries) require different lineups. The $12 can afford to be slightly more chalk-leaning with a single good swing. The $5 needs genuine differentiation across 2-3 axes.

## The Anchor Problem

In SE, your conviction plays must be **tight pairs or singles, not spreads.** You pick 2-3 fighters you believe in deeply, and you build the rest of the lineup to *support* those anchors, not to compete with them.

The 5/16/26 SEs both locked Santos + Gantt as common anchors. This was correct conviction execution. But then we filled the rest of the lineup with different fighters (different lever, different salary saver). **The problem: when both common anchors failed, the diversification on the other 4 fighters didn't matter.** Both lineups were dead in the same way.

**Better SE design:** If you believe in Santos + Gantt, make that your core and build 2-3 different lineups around that core with different leverages. Don't try to "diversify away" from your convictions by changing the low-conviction slots. You can't.

Or: Believe deeply in only ONE anchor (Santos or Gantt, not both), build the lever around that one, and let the rest of the lineup be flexibility.

## SE Diversification Rules

You cannot diversify a SE the way you diversify an MME. But you can structure TWO SEs (if playing two in the same slate) to hedge risk across uncorrelated outcomes.

True diversification across two SEs requires differing on **at least one high-conviction play**, not just on the low-owned leverage piece. If both SEs have Santos + Gantt, they're the same lineup twice — different salary savers don't matter.

Two SEs should differ on:
- **At minimum:** One main conviction play (one has Santos, the other doesn't, or one has Gantt, the other doesn't)
- **Ideally:** Different binary-fight sides (one plays Erslan/Tokkos, the other plays the leverage from a different fight)

This means accepting that one SE might underweight a play you believe in, in order to hedge against common-anchor failure. This is the inverse of MME, where you can afford to have 100+ lineups all-in on Santos+Gantt.

## Process Over Outcome

SE is pure variance amplification. One lineup, one outcome, one set of 6 fighters. The variance is high.

**Process in SE means:** Did you build a lineup with a realistic path to winning? Did the lineup have 2-3 conviction anchors? Did it have one genuine leverage swing? Did you diversify the SE from your other SE if playing multiple? Did you verify the submission before lock?

**Outcome in SE is:** Did this specific 6-fighter combination hit hard enough to cash in this specific field size?

Process matters. But even perfect process loses SEs when the slate breaks in a way your lineup didn't anticipate. Don't confuse a loss with a process error unless the autopsy shows the process was actually wrong.

## Ceiling Threshold Discipline

Before locking a SE lineup, sum the win-case projections for all 6 fighters. If the total is under 550 in a 490-entrant field (or under 600 in a 1,500-entrant field), the lineup's ceiling is structurally insufficient to win.

**Cash is not the goal of SE.** SE prize structures are top-heavy — you might cash at 22-25% of the field but the bottom 50% of cashes earn back roughly the buy-in. The real money is top-5%, which requires lineups built to reach 600+ points.

**The 5/16/26 SEs had ceilings of 572 and 581 in a field where the winner scored 660.81.** Even with perfect variance, neither lineup could have won. They were built to cash, not to win. That's a structural error.

## Bankroll Reality

SEs are high-variance. A 50% ROI process over 30 slates might go 0-for-5 to start, then 8-for-10, then 2-for-3. This is normal variance.

**Single Entry requires bankroll discipline.** If you're playing $12 and $5 SEs, that's $17 per slate. Over 10 slates, that's $170. If your first 5 slates all lose (possible with 22-25% cash rates), you've spent $85 with zero return. You need to be comfortable with that variance.

Alternatively: Play SEs as a **smaller-cap companion** to a larger MME volume. If MME is your core $75/slate project, SEs should be $5-10/slate maximum, treated as exploration, not as a primary income source.

## SE is Not a Backup to MME

This is the biggest mistake: treating SE as "the same game as MME, just with one lineup instead of 150."

They are different games. Different field dynamics. Different variance. Different process.

If you don't believe in the SE process enough to treat it as its own sport with its own framework, don't play SEs. If you do, commit to learning it, documenting it, and playing it enough (10+ slates) to know whether you have an edge.

Mixing MME + SE as "one bankroll" without separate frameworks is a fast way to lose the confidence to play either one well.
