### Lessons That Generalize

1. **The 50% Chalk Rule.** Drivers at 50%+ ownership at lock deserve at least 33% portfolio exposure. The market rarely is that wrong about price/start math. Fade only on definitive evidence (suspension, withdrawal, mechanical issue, confirmed equipment problem). **Backup car alone is not enough.**

2. **The Anchor vs. Pivot Distinction.** Sub-15% owned plays are pivots — fine to use, but never as a lineup's load-bearing piece. The lineup's structural integrity should hold even if the leverage play busts. Test before lock: "If this 10%-owned play scores zero, can this lineup still cash?"

3. **Correlation ≠ Substitution.** Two plays addressing similar theses (chaos PD, HMS speed, etc.) are not interchangeable. They are independent shots at different point distributions. AD and Bubba both being deep-PD plays meant they could BOTH hit, not that one substituted for the other.

4. **HMS Intermediate Correlation Confirmed.** Byron + Elliott double-up worked at Texas. Track this for Kansas, Vegas, Charlotte, future intermediates.

5. **Sub-10% Punts > 15-20% Punts (when floors match).** Ty Dillon at 7% (29 pts) vs. Zane at 17% (33 pts) — similar output, vastly more leverage. Default to lowest-owned of viable Tier 5 punts.

6. **Identified Portfolio Gaps Get Addressed Pre-Lock.** We flagged Suarez as a hole and didn't fix it. Suarez scored 35.35 pts. If a gap is identified, find a way to address it in at least one lineup, even if construction is awkward.

### Slate-Specific Notes (Don't Generalize)

- Larson scoring -16 was a true outlier event (loose wheel + crash combo). Don't draw broad conclusions about HMS dominators from this race.
- Briscoe's bust at 10% own was driver-specific, not a leverage-tier indictment. Briscoe specifically had off-pace race; the leverage tier as a category isn't broken.
- Backup-car Bubba surviving and finishing top 5 was a unique data point. Don't *assume* backup cars always survive going forward — but also don't assume they don't.
- Riley Herbst at 3.27% own scoring 40.6 was a true low-probability hit. Punt-tier outliers of that magnitude shouldn't be modeled into base case.

### Process Notes

- **Held lineups through two ownership update rounds.** When Blaney dropped 54%→39%, it should have prompted reconsideration of the whole chalk fade structure (since the Bubba fade was justified relative to the Blaney fade). Instead, we treated it as confirmation of existing plan. **Lesson: Major ownership shifts should trigger full re-evaluation, not partial.**

- **Lock-time discipline failure on L2.** When L2 was constructed around Briscoe as a load-bearing leverage piece, that should have triggered the "no single load-bearing leverage" rule. We knew the rule and built the lineup against it anyway because we were committed to the contrarian thesis.

- **The Bubba decision was made under high confidence.** I (Claude) used phrases like "stay at 0 of 3" and "the Bubba fade is real risk but defensible." That confidence wasn't earned. The fade thesis was "AD covers same bet" + "backup car risk" — both of which were hedges, not positive evidence. **Future calls of this magnitude need calibrated language and explicit acknowledgment of downside scenario.**

### Lessons That Generalize

1. **The thesis was wrong, not the construction.** Five builds of optimizer tuning cannot save a portfolio whose foundational thesis is incorrect. **Before iterating on Min/Max exposures, validate the core strategic thesis.** "Zilisch wins if SVG bleeds laps" was a hedge dressed up as positive evidence — exactly the philosophy.md anti-pattern.

2. **The "Absence of Confidence" trap (philosophy.md, reinforced).** Our Zilisch overweight was based on SVG having a *capped ceiling*, not Zilisch having a *positive win path*. When SVG's ceiling wasn't capped (he scored 78), the entire thesis collapsed. **Future rule: If the contrarian dom thesis depends on the chalk dom under-performing, it's an absence of confidence thesis. Default to field rate on the chalk and re-think the contrarian.**

3. **Correlated leverage stacks compound bust risk (extending Texas Bell+Zilisch lesson).** Our Bell + Reddick + Wallace + Briscoe Toyota overweight + Zilisch overweight was 5 drivers all betting on the same outcome: "Toyota speed + Spire McDowell beats SVG/HMS chalk path." When Bell and Zilisch busted simultaneously, the entire portfolio took a hit. **Future rule: Identify the thesis behind each overweight. If 3+ overweights share the same thesis, that's a single bet, not diversified leverage.**

4. **Sub-10% own + sub-$6,000 = real win path at road courses (extending Texas Ty Dillon lesson).** Austin Dillon at 5% field own / $5,800 / 57.9 pts was the slate-winning punt. The Texas lesson holds: sub-10% punts > 15-20% punts when floors match. **Confirmed at two consecutive slates.**

5. **Iteration tourism (newly defined).** Builds 4-7 were marginal optimization on a portfolio whose strategic thesis was already locked in by Build 3. Each iteration solved one micro-problem and spawned another (Reddick fix → SVG break → Larson fix → Keselowski break → AD fix → SVG weaken). **Future rule: After Build 3, no more iterations unless a strategic thesis changes. Optimizer noise is not optimization.**

6. **Lock-time discipline rule needs to bite at Build 3, not Build 6.** Texas lesson was about not holding lineups through ownership updates. This slate's lesson is the inverse: not running additional builds after the strategy is locked. Both are forms of the same failure — letting discomfort drive iteration.

7. **Field consensus at extremes is even stronger than we modeled.** SVG at 60% was correct. Preece at 41% was correct. The slate's optimal lineup was 4/6 chalk + 1 mid-chalk + 1 punt. **Future rule: When chalk is structurally sound (price/start/track-fit all aligned), the 50% Chalk Rule may need to expand toward field rate, not below it.**

8. **Process disagreement when articulated saves money even when outcome doesn't validate.** I (Claude) refused Build 7 analysis and forced the lock decision on Build 5. The outcome would have been similar regardless of build (Bell/Zilisch dominated the loss), but **the discipline of forcing the lock prevented Build 8/9/10 and preserved decision integrity.** Maintain this even when frustrating.

### Slate-Specific Notes (Don't Generalize)

- **Austin Dillon at 57.90 from P25** — Glen-specific. Don't assume 5%-owned AD always hits.
- **JHN at 41.90 pts at 3.7% own** — true low-probability outlier. Don't model into base case.
- **Bell finishing outside top 20 from P8** — driver-specific failure on the day, not Toyota-tier indictment (Reddick 50.8, Briscoe 45.45 confirm Toyotas were fast).
- **Zilisch at 13.85 pts despite being structurally elite** — driver-specific bust. Don't infer "Zilisch is bad on road courses" from one race.
- **Kyle Busch hitting from P21 with 49.45 pts** — confirms Dustin's mid-pack track-position thesis. May generalize.

### Process Notes

- **Seven-build iteration sequence was excessive.** Builds 1-3 were genuine problem-solving (Reddick min, SVG floor, Larson min). Builds 4-7 were iteration tourism. **Build 3 should have been the lock candidate.** The structural thesis was fixed by then; further changes were rearranging deck chairs.

- **The "I just wanted to build one more set" moment was correctly identified and called out.** Ryan was honest about it; Claude held the line. **This is the model for future lock-time discipline.** When iteration is driven by discomfort rather than diagnosed problem, the answer is "lock the current build."

- **Build comparison fatigue is real and dangerous.** By Build 5/6/7, the optimizer outputs looked similar enough that small changes felt meaningful but weren't. **Future rule: If three consecutive builds satisfy the same strategic criteria, the marginal differences are noise. Lock the first satisfying build.**

- **Calibrated confidence ("80% confident in Build 5") was technically correct but functionally wrong.** The 20% downside (SVG dominates) was acknowledged but bet sizing didn't reflect 20% probability — Zilisch at 47% was a 50%+ commitment to the 80% scenario. **Future rule: When acknowledging downside scenarios, bet size must reflect the probability of inverse, not the probability of base case.**

- **The Zilisch overweight came from the Step 1 track profile, not from the projection sheet.** This is normally correct — track profile beats raw projections. But in this case, the track profile correctly identified Zilisch as having a positive win path; we over-extrapolated from "viable win path" to "primary slate-winning thesis." **Future rule: Track-profile-derived overweights should be capped at +10pts above field rate, not +20pts.**
