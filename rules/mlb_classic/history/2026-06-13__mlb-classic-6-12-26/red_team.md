**Attacks:**

1. **The band breach is real and its justification is fabricated.** The codified pivot-budget lesson (3 mechanism confirmations) prescribes ~15–18% avg own and 3–5 sub-10% plays. L1 sits at **10.35% avg own with 6 sub-10% plays**. The portfolio audit's defense — "above the 3–5 note, matching the 6/11 ledger-best build exactly" — does not survive a read of the archive: the 6/11 top-4.4% lineup had **four** sub-10% plays (Young 6.1, Semien 4.8, Bichette 5.2, Benge 7.0) at **16.8%** avg own, inside the band, and its own audit said so explicitly. The only logged below-band build is 6/9 (8 sub-10% plays, "double the winners' density") — a miss. L1 at 6 sub-10s sits between the codified band and the known failure profile, defended with a citation that's wrong. The mechanism matters: outside the MIL-4 axis, Imai (7.7%) has codified cover as the value-P2/salary-enabler, but **Slater (6.9%) is a second discretionary leverage piece with no cover** — that's layering, the exact thing the lesson exists to stop.
2. **The MIL bats just busted as a group.** The park file's 6/10 observation: Bauers 0.0, Contreras a top underperformer, in a 7.35-implied game. Variance, not mechanism (GPP guard applies — not a fade argument), but the thesis pays $18.0K for four bats projecting 6.4–9.5; the blowup has to actually arrive.
3. **Condensation could land on Misiorowski.** Both validated condensation events (Detmers 38→69%, Scott 44→56–63%) condensed onto the projected-own **leader** — which today is Misiorowski (25.2 vs Yesavage 23.6). The analysis's affordability argument for why Yesavage condenses instead is plausible but unvalidated. If the field condenses on Misiorowski, L1 holds a 40%+ owned arm — survivable (he's the highest-projected arm at -260) but the "the field CAN'T condense on him" clause in the thesis is asserted, not evidenced.

**Leverage audit:** MIL-4 at 1.4/1.8/1.5/2.7% — genuinely contrarian, the real thing. Misiorowski 25.2% and Imai 7.7% verified against the bundle's pitcher table. No chalk-in-costume here; the problem is the opposite — one more leverage piece than the budget allows.

The portfolio was built for a field that hasn't been named. If these entries go into a large-field GPP, the band logic and the condensation sizing both lose their evidentiary basis. If ATH posts a crooked number, the field's abundant ATH-4/5 stacks beat both entries simultaneously — this is the single game-state that kills the whole portfolio. It's a deliberate stance, but it rests on ONE park-file observation (6/10, self-labeled "a lean, not a rule"). The analysis's own Decision 1 called the whole-game fade "the most binary fade on the board — underweight, don't zero"; pieces-only across both lineups is closer to zero than to underweight on the primary side. Defensible in a 2-lineup portfolio — but own it as the portfolio's biggest bet, because it is.

But Slater is in a **projected** TB lineup (9:38 PM game) in both entries: one scratch forces two edits near lock. Distinct-question check passes cleanly: L1 wins where chalk-arm scoring holds, L2 where it doesn't.
6. **Condensation asymmetry cuts both ways.** If the field condenses on Misiorowski (the projected-own leader, where it landed both prior slates), L1 holds the condensed arm (neutral-to-fine) and L2's fade halves in value. If it condenses on Yesavage as sized, both lineups gain.
