# PGA Classic Philosophy

*Last updated: 2026-05-19*

The foundational principles that govern decision-making. These are the *why* behind the Framework. They should change rarely, if ever — and only with extensive evidence. When a tactical question doesn't have a clear answer in the Framework, return here.

---

## 1. Process Is the Only Thing You Control

Every lineup is a probability distribution. The outcome that materializes is one sample from that distribution. A losing week with sound process is informationally identical to a winning week with sound process — both confirm that the process was correctly calibrated. A winning week with bad process is the most dangerous outcome of all: it teaches the wrong lesson and produces overconfidence.

**The job is to maximize expected value per dollar entered, measured across hundreds of slates — not to win this week.** Outcome-tracking is necessary for diagnosis, but outcome-praising or outcome-blaming is forbidden in the autopsy.

When reviewing a week, the question is never *did this win?* The question is *would I make this same decision again given the information I had at the time?*

## 2. The Field Is Wrong in Specific, Identifiable Ways

DFS GPP edge does not come from being smarter than the field about who will play well. It comes from being correctly positioned when the field is wrong about *who they will play*. These are different problems.

The field's predictable mistakes:
- Overweighting recent results (especially last-week winners)
- Overweighting narrative (course history, "home state," "due for a win")
- Underweighting model projections when they conflict with narrative
- Crowding on the same chalk because of analyst convergence
- Avoiding players coming off poor finishes even when underlying numbers are strong

The edge is not "this player will score well." The edge is "this player will score well at a price the field has mis-owned."

## 3. Variance Is the Game, Not the Obstacle

PGA DFS is structurally high-variance. 70-man fields, no head-to-head matchup correlations, cut/no-cut binary outcomes, and individual sport variance all stack. Long losing streaks are mathematically expected, not signs of process failure.

**A 1-of-4 cash week with sound process is a normal result.** A 4-of-4 cash week with sound process is a positive variance outlier. Neither should change tactical decisions. The framework is built for expected value across sample size, not for any specific week.

The corollary: if a process change is being considered, the trigger should be a pattern across 8+ weeks, not a reaction to one outcome.

## 4. The Three Failure Modes

Every losing week falls into one of three categories. Diagnose correctly before adjusting.

**Variance loss:** Sound process, bad sample. The model was right, the field was wrong, the players you correctly identified didn't perform. No adjustment needed.

**Process error:** A specific decision violated the framework or relied on faulty reasoning. The adjustment is to fix the reasoning, not to change the player.

**Blind spot:** A pattern you weren't looking for produced the outcome. The adjustment is to expand what you track, not to over-fit to this week. (Truist Aberg miss and PGA Championship Smalley/Rai miss are both blind-spot losses.)

Most losing weeks are variance losses. The discipline is to resist treating them as process errors.

## 5. Chalk Has a Purpose

Fading chalk for its own sake is as much a failure mode as blindly playing it. Chalk exists because chalk is often correct. The job is to distinguish *trap chalk* (overowned relative to projection or model edge) from *value chalk* (correctly priced and correctly owned, just popular).

**Trap chalk is the easiest fade in the portfolio.** Value chalk is the hardest fade — and full-fading it is usually a structural mistake. If the model says a player is the best on the slate and the field plays them at expected levels, at least one lineup in the portfolio should carry them as a hedge.

The cost of being wrong about value chalk (zero exposure when they win) is higher than the cost of being wrong about trap chalk (small exposure when they win). Asymmetry rules favor the hedge.

**A fade is conditional on the ownership materializing.** Before locking any coffin/writer fade, sanity-check the player's actual/late ownership read: if it is sub-~12%, the fade premise is void and the player is mispriced leverage, not trap chalk. A sub-10% "fade" is not a fade — it just zeroes cheap upside. (Repeatedly cost slate-definers: Reitan/Clark at the Memorial, Reitan at the RBC, Fitzpatrick at the Travelers.)

## 6. Leverage Is Asymmetric Bet Construction

A coffin-list overweight at sub-15% ownership is structurally equivalent to a chalk play at 25%+ ownership in terms of ceiling outcome — but with materially lower duplication risk. This is the core leverage trade.

The leverage spine isn't "be contrarian." Contrarian play that doesn't survive a 70-man field's variance still loses. The leverage spine is "find the plays where the model edge is largest *and* the field ownership is smallest, and double down on them."

When the coffin list offers two such plays on the same slate, deploying both together is multiplicative, not additive.

## 7. The Portfolio Is the Unit of Analysis

Single lineups don't make decisions; portfolios do. Even in single-entry play, the portfolio is the multi-week sequence of decisions, not the one lineup. In multi-entry formats, the portfolio is the set of lineups submitted across all contests on a slate.

A portfolio distributes risk across multiple slate outcomes:
- The chalk-wins outcome
- The contrarian-wins outcome
- The chaos outcome (low-projection player wins outright)
- The expected-distribution outcome

Each lineup or weekly decision should be a *distinct angle* on the slate, not a variant of one thesis.

The corollary: if all entries miss cash, the question is not "was my read wrong?" — it is "did my portfolio cover enough of the outcome space?"

## 8. Don't Fight Your Own Framework

When the framework outputs a build you find uncomfortable, the framework wins. Discomfort usually means: (a) the build looks different from your prior reads, or (b) the build looks different from the field consensus. Both are features, not bugs.

If after multiple weeks the framework produces builds you consistently distrust, the framework needs reform — not the individual builds. Reform happens deliberately, between weeks, with evidence from the Autopsies. Never mid-build.

## 9. Sample Size Humility

Eight weeks of DFS data is not statistically meaningful. Twenty weeks is barely indicative. Treat every conclusion as provisional, especially conclusions about individual player performance trends, "course types," and "weather edges." The Framework can be updated; the Philosophy moves slowly.

The strongest update signal is when the same blind spot or process error appears in 3+ autopsies independently. One-off patterns are usually noise.

## 10. The Long Game

GPP play is not a salary; it is an investment portfolio with a deep negative-skew distribution. The expected return is dominated by a small number of high-finish weeks. Most weeks lose money. A correctly executed long-term GPP strategy will produce 10–20% cash rates in SE, with the entire profit driven by 1–2 top-1% finishes per quarter.

This means:
- Bankroll management is non-negotiable. Never risk more than 2–3% of bankroll per slate.
- Tilt management is non-negotiable. The week after a deep finish is the most dangerous week.
- Patience is non-negotiable. The framework was built for the average week, not this week.

The work is the process. The wins are the consequence.

## 11. Quality of Rules Over Quantity (Cross-Sport Operating Principle)

Across all DFS GPP projects (PGA, NFL, NBA, MLB, future sports), the same rule-construction discipline applies. Use the fewest, sharpest constraints that produce the desired structural outcome — not a comprehensive rule set. Each rule must earn its place by addressing a specific failure mode.

If a build needs more than 3–5 core constraints, the strategy is wrong, not the rules. Layering 3+ rules at once obscures which one solved the problem and which one created the next one. The slate read happens first; the rules express the read; the optimizer (or single-lineup build) executes. Not the other way around.

Two-build maximum per slate: one conviction baseline, one refined version. Stop after that.
